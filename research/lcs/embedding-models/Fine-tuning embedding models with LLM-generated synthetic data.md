# Fine-tuning embedding models with LLM-generated synthetic data

**Synthetic query generation has emerged as the most cost-effective method to adapt general-purpose embedding models to domain-specific retrieval**, delivering 5–10% improvement in nDCG@10 for as little as $1–55 in compute and API costs. The technique, pioneered by systems like Promptagator, InPars, and GPL between 2022 and 2023, uses large language models to manufacture training pairs—synthetic queries paired with real corpus passages—that teach a retriever the semantic vocabulary of a target domain. For practitioners building RAG systems on technical corpora, synthetic fine-tuning now represents a compelling middle path between using a generic off-the-shelf model and paying for a premium embedding API. The approach works because contrastive learning on even a few thousand well-constructed pairs shifts the embedding space enough to capture domain-specific relevance signals that pre-trained models miss.

## Promptagator and its descendants showed the way

The [Promptagator paper (Dai et al., 2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.11755) introduced a method that transforms as few as **8 annotated query-document examples** into millions of synthetic training pairs. The system feeds documents from the target corpus into [FLAN 137B](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.11416), an instruction-tuned LLM, using task-specific prompt templates. For each document, the LLM generates up to 8 synthetic queries via sampling decoding at temperature 0.7. The prompt format embeds task-specific descriptions—for instance, on the ArguAna dataset, a document is prefixed with "Argument:" and the model generates a "Counter Argument:" as the query. Across 11 BEIR benchmark datasets, the system generated **58.46 million queries** from roughly 1 million sampled documents per dataset.

The critical innovation is **round-trip consistency filtering**. After generating all synthetic pairs, Promptagator trains an initial dual encoder on the unfiltered data, then uses that retriever to check whether the original document appears in the top-1 retrieval results for each synthetic query. Only pairs that pass this round-trip test are retained. This self-filtering step improved average nDCG@10 by [2.5 points across all BEIR datasets](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2209.11755), primarily by eliminating overly generic queries that match many documents and hallucinated queries irrelevant to the source passage.

The results are striking. Promptagator's few-shot dual encoder (just **110M parameters**, initialized from T5-base) achieved **47.8 nDCG@10** averaged across 11 BEIR datasets—outperforming ColBERT v2 (46.2), SPLADE v2 (46.6), and GTR-XXL (44.9, a 6B-parameter model trained on MS MARCO). When combined with a reranker in the Promptagator++ configuration, performance jumped to **52.8 nDCG@10**, surpassing even monoT5-3B at 51.1. On individual datasets, gains were dramatic: ArguAna improved from ColBERT v2's 46.3 to 59.4, and Touché-2020 from 26.3 to 34.5. Perhaps most remarkably, the paper showed that [8 examples plus LLM generation matched the performance of approximately 50,000 supervised examples](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2209.11755) on MS MARCO.

Two parallel approaches arrived at similar conclusions through different mechanisms. [InPars (Bonifacio et al., 2022)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3477495.3531863) used GPT-3 Curie with 3-shot prompting to generate 100,000 queries per dataset at a cost of **$100–300 USD**, then aggressively filtered to the top 10,000 by normalized log probability. The [InPars-v2 follow-up](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.01820) replaced GPT-3 with open-source GPT-J and used a cross-encoder reranker for filtering, achieving state-of-the-art results on BEIR. Meanwhile, [GPL (Wang et al., 2022)](https://aclanthology.org/2022.naacl-main.168/) took a different route entirely: it used DocT5Query to generate ~250K queries per dataset, then employed a cross-encoder to produce continuous pseudo-labels rather than binary relevance judgments. GPL's **MarginMSE loss**—which trains the retriever to mimic the cross-encoder's score margins between positive and negative passages—proved remarkably robust: even with nearly random query generation (temperature 10), GPL still improved over zero-shot baselines by ~3 nDCG@10 points. The TSDAE+GPL configuration achieved **52.9 average nDCG@10** on domain-specific BEIR subsets, a **7.7-point improvement** over the zero-shot MS MARCO baseline.

## A practitioner's recipe for synthetic fine-tuning

The practical pipeline for fine-tuning an embedding model with synthetic data has converged on a well-defined set of steps, documented across the [Sentence Transformers training guide](https://sbert.net/docs/sentence_transformer/training_overview.html), the [FlagEmbedding fine-tuning documentation](https://github.com/FlagOpen/FlagEmbedding), and the [LlamaIndex embedding fine-tuning tutorial](https://www.llamaindex.ai/blog/fine-tuning-embeddings-for-rag-with-synthetic-data-e534409a3971).

**Step 1: Chunk the corpus.** Split documents into passages of 200–512 tokens. These passages become the "positive" documents in training pairs. Standard chunking strategies apply—overlap of 50–100 tokens between chunks prevents information loss at boundaries.

**Step 2: Generate synthetic queries.** For each passage, prompt an LLM to generate 2–4 questions that the passage could answer. The [LlamaIndex tutorial](https://docs.llamaindex.ai/en/stable/examples/finetuning/embeddings/finetune_embedding/) uses a prompt template instructing the model to act as a "Teacher/Professor" setting quiz questions restricted to the provided context. Temperature 0 is typical for deterministic output, though Promptagator's temperature 0.7 with multiple samples per passage produces more diverse queries. GPT-4o-mini and GPT-3.5-turbo are the most cost-effective generators—[Phil Schmid's HuggingFace study](https://www.philschmid.de/fine-tune-embedding-model-for-rag) used just **6,300 synthetic pairs** to achieve a ~7% nDCG@10 improvement on financial documents.

**Step 3: Mine hard negatives.** The [FlagEmbedding library](https://github.com/FlagOpen/FlagEmbedding) provides a built-in hard negative mining script that retrieves top-200 passages for each synthetic query using the base embedding model, then samples 7–15 negatives from ranks 2–200. This range matters: negatives that are too easy (random passages) provide weak training signal, while negatives that are too hard (rank 1–2) are often false negatives. Research from the [NV-Retriever paper](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2407.15831) found that roughly **70% of the most similar passages** mined as negatives from MS MARCO were actually relevant, making false-negative filtering essential.

**Step 4: Train with contrastive loss.** [MultipleNegativesRankingLoss (MNRL)](https://sbert.net/docs/sentence_transformer/loss_overview.html) is the standard choice—an InfoNCE loss that treats other in-batch positives as negatives. The Sentence Transformers documentation recommends batch sizes of **64 or larger** (more in-batch negatives improve contrastive learning), learning rates of **1e-5 to 2e-5**, and training for **2–5 epochs**. The `BatchSamplers.NO_DUPLICATES` sampler prevents degenerate batches where identical passages appear. For BGE models specifically, [the training configuration](https://blog-en.sionic.ai/flag-embedding) uses temperature 0.02, `negatives_cross_device` for distributed training, and query/passage max lengths of 64/256 tokens.

**Data volume requirements are surprisingly modest.** The LlamaIndex tutorial achieved its 6-point hit-rate improvement with a few hundred synthetic pairs generated from financial PDF chunks, and [training ran on an M2 MacBook Pro](https://www.llamaindex.ai/blog/fine-tuning-embeddings-for-rag-with-synthetic-data-e534409a3971) without a GPU. Phil Schmid's study needed only 6,300 pairs and **3 minutes of training** on a consumer GPU. The GPL paper found that performance saturated after ~100K training steps with ~250K synthetic queries, and that [50K passages was sufficient](https://aclanthology.org/2022.naacl-main.168/) for near-optimal performance. For most domain-adaptation scenarios, **5,000–10,000 high-quality synthetic pairs** represent the practical sweet spot.

## Quality controls that prevent degenerate training

Synthetic data is noisy by nature—LLMs hallucinate details, generate overly generic questions, or produce queries answerable by many passages. Without quality controls, fine-tuning on such data can degrade retrieval performance. The research literature has converged on several essential techniques.

**LLM-as-judge filtering** is the most accessible approach. The [Databricks fine-tuning study](https://www.databricks.com/blog/improving-retrieval-and-rag-embedding-model-finetuning) used GPT-4o to evaluate each synthetic query for relevance and specificity before including it in the training set. A cheaper variant from the [Innowhyte.ai guide](https://www.innowhyte.ai/blogs/end-to-end-embeddings-fine-tuning-guide) instructs the generator to output "None" for uninformative chunks (metadata, boilerplate, contact information), preventing garbage-in training pairs.

**Cross-encoder scoring** provides a more principled filter. InPars-v2 uses [monoT5-3B to score all 100K synthetic pairs and keeps only the top 10K](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.01820)—discarding 90% of generated data. GPL takes this further by using cross-encoder scores as continuous training labels rather than binary relevance, which makes the system inherently robust to low-quality queries: poorly generated queries receive low cross-encoder scores and contribute minimal gradient signal.

**False negative mitigation** is critical when using hard negative mining. The [GISTEmbedLoss](https://sbert.net/docs/sentence_transformer/loss_overview.html) in Sentence Transformers uses a guide model to identify and ignore false negatives within each batch. The NV-Retriever paper's [TopK-PercPos method](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2407.15831) sets a maximum threshold for negative scores at 95% of the positive score—any "negative" scoring above this threshold is treated as a likely false negative and excluded.

**Dynamic hard negative refresh** addresses the problem of negatives becoming stale during training. The [Conan-Embedding approach](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.15710v2) checks every 100 iterations whether the average score of hard negatives has dropped significantly relative to initial values, and if so, re-mines negatives aligned with the current model weights.

Finally, **weight merging** via [LM-Cocktail](https://github.com/FlagOpen/FlagEmbedding) provides a post-training safety net against catastrophic forgetting. By interpolating fine-tuned and original model weights (typically 50/50), practitioners preserve general-purpose retrieval capability while gaining domain-specific improvements. The [AWS fine-tuning guide](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/machine-learning/fine-tune-a-bge-embedding-model-using-synthetic-data-from-amazon-bedrock/) specifically recommends this approach when deploying fine-tuned BGE models.

## When to fine-tune versus when to upgrade the model

The cost-benefit calculation depends heavily on the domain gap between general web text and the target corpus. The evidence supports a clear decision framework.

**Synthetic fine-tuning excels when retrieval is the bottleneck.** The Databricks study found that [fine-tuned gte-large-en-v1.5 outperformed unfine-tuned OpenAI text-embedding-3-large](https://www.databricks.com/blog/improving-retrieval-and-rag-embedding-model-finetuning) on domain-specific financial and manufacturing datasets. The LlamaIndex tutorial showed that fine-tuned bge-small-en (137M parameters, free) reached **84% hit-rate**, closing most of the gap to OpenAI's Ada-002 at 87%—[for essentially zero infrastructure cost](https://www.llamaindex.ai/blog/fine-tuning-embeddings-for-rag-with-synthetic-data-e534409a3971). Phil Schmid demonstrated that fine-tuning with [Matryoshka loss](https://www.philschmid.de/fine-tune-embedding-model-for-rag) at 128 dimensions outperformed the baseline at 768 dimensions by 6.51%, delivering better performance at **6x less storage**.

The total cost of a synthetic fine-tuning pipeline is remarkably low: **$1–5** for generating 6K pairs with GPT-3.5-turbo, **$10–50** if using GPT-4o with judge-based filtering, and effectively **$0–5** for the training compute itself. The [SPEED framework](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.18634v1) further demonstrated that using a fine-tuned 8B open-source model for generation achieves GPT-4-level quality at less than 1/10th the API cost, generating 920K training samples after deduplication.

**Upgrading the model wins when domain specialization already exists.** [Voyage-code-3](https://blog.voyageai.com/2024/12/04/voyage-code-3/) outperforms OpenAI text-embedding-3-large by **13.80% average** across 32 code retrieval datasets—a gap that synthetic fine-tuning of a general model is unlikely to close. Similarly, [Voyage-3-large](https://blog.voyageai.com/2025/01/07/voyage-3-large/) outperforms OpenAI-v3-large by 9.74% across 100 datasets spanning 8 domains, with 512-dimensional binary embeddings still beating OpenAI's 3072-dimensional float embeddings by 1.16%. When a domain-specialized commercial model exists, the marginal benefit of synthetic fine-tuning typically does not justify building a training pipeline.

The break-even point on infrastructure also matters. At current [Voyage pricing](https://docs.voyageai.com/docs/pricing) of $0.06–0.18 per million tokens, API-based embedding is cheaper than self-hosting until roughly **500M–1B tokens per month**, at which point a self-hosted fine-tuned model on a ~$100/month GPU becomes more economical. However, as [Weaviate's decision guide](https://weaviate.io/blog/fine-tune-embedding-model) notes, changing embedding models requires re-indexing all data—a non-trivial operational cost that favors fine-tuning the existing model over switching to a new one.

The Databricks study identified one crucial caveat: [when retrieval was already strong on their DocsQA benchmark, fine-tuning provided negligible improvement](https://www.databricks.com/blog/improving-retrieval-and-rag-embedding-model-finetuning). This suggests practitioners should first diagnose whether retrieval quality is actually the bottleneck in their RAG pipeline before investing in fine-tuning.

## Conclusion

Synthetic fine-tuning of embedding models has matured from a research technique into a production-ready practice. The academic foundations—Promptagator's round-trip filtering, GPL's continuous pseudo-labels, InPars's aggressive score-based selection—have been distilled into accessible tooling through Sentence Transformers, FlagEmbedding, and LlamaIndex. The practical recipe is now well-established: generate 5,000–10,000 synthetic query-passage pairs using any capable LLM, mine hard negatives from the base model's top-200 retrievals, train with MNRL for 2–5 epochs, and optionally merge weights to preserve generalization. The entire process costs under $50 and trains in minutes. Where this approach will not help is when a domain-specialized commercial model already captures the relevant semantic space, or when retrieval is not the actual bottleneck. The decision to fine-tune versus upgrade should be driven by diagnosing the specific failure modes of the current retrieval system on representative queries—not by benchmark scores alone, which [Pinecone has warned may be inflated](https://www.pinecone.io/learn/series/rag/embedding-models-rundown/) for models trained on MTEB evaluation data.
