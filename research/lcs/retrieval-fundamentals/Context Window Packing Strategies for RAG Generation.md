# Context Window Packing Strategies for RAG Generation

**The order, quantity, and formatting of retrieved chunks inside an LLM's context window can swing answer accuracy by more than 30 percentage points**, yet most RAG pipelines still stuff chunks in retriever-ranked order and hope for the best. Empirical research — from Stanford's landmark "Lost in the Middle" study through 2025 EMNLP work on real retrieval pipelines — reveals a nuanced picture: position bias is real but its practical impact in production RAG may be smaller than the academic findings suggest. Meanwhile, production frameworks like LlamaIndex and LangChain have built sophisticated context assembly machinery that goes well beyond naive concatenation, and emerging compression techniques can deliver equivalent answer quality at a fraction of the token cost.

This report synthesizes empirical evidence on three interrelated problems: how chunk ordering interacts with LLM attention patterns, how production frameworks assemble context windows, and what metadata overhead actually costs in practice.

## The U-shaped curve that launched a thousand reorderers

The foundational work on position bias is [Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.03172) by Nelson F. Liu et al., published in *Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics* (2024, Vol. 12, pp. 157–173). The study tested six models — GPT-3.5-Turbo (4K and 16K), Claude-1.3 (8K and 100K), MPT-30B-Instruct, and LongChat-13B-16K — on two tasks: multi-document question answering (MDQA) using 2,655 NaturalQuestions queries with up to 30 Wikipedia passages, and a synthetic key-value retrieval task with up to 300 JSON entries.

The central finding is stark. When a relevant document sits in the **middle positions (roughly positions 8–12 of 20)**, GPT-3.5-Turbo's accuracy drops by **more than 20 percentage points** compared to when the same document appears at position 1 or position 20. More alarmingly, mid-context performance falls *below closed-book accuracy* (**56.1%**), meaning the retrieved documents actively hurt the model. The performance curve forms a distinctive U-shape: strong at the beginning, strong at the end, degraded in the middle. Extended-context variants showed no improvement — GPT-3.5-Turbo and GPT-3.5-Turbo-16K produced nearly identical curves when input fit both windows, and the same held for [Claude-1.3 versus Claude-1.3-100K](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.03172). Adding more documents yielded diminishing returns: jumping from 20 to 50 passages improved GPT-3.5-Turbo accuracy by only ~1.5%.

Two mitigations showed promise. **Query-aware contextualization** — placing the query both before and after the document block — achieved near-perfect key-value retrieval but barely moved MDQA accuracy. Encoder-decoder architectures (Flan-UL2) proved more robust, showing only a **1.9% absolute difference** between best and worst positions within training-length contexts, suggesting the U-shaped bias is partly an artifact of decoder-only autoregressive training.

## Real RAG pipelines complicate the picture

The clean experimental setup of Liu et al. uses exactly one relevant document among controlled distractors. Real retrieval pipelines are messier, and [a 2025 EMNLP study by Cuconasu et al.](https://aclanthology.org/2025.emnlp-main.1422.pdf) — pointedly titled "Do RAG Systems Really Suffer From Positional Bias?" — argues the practical impact is **marginal**. Testing Llama 3.2 3B, Llama 3.1 8B, Llama 3.3 70B, and Qwen 2.5 7B across PopQA, NaturalQuestions, and TriviaQA with real BM25 and BGE retrievers, they found that sophisticated reordering strategies perform within **1–3 accuracy points of random shuffling**. With BGE plus reranking and k=5, sequential ordering scored 68.53 on Qwen 7B while the position-optimized "MaxRelevance" strategy scored 71.73 — a gap that random shuffling (71.00) nearly closed.

The explanation is elegant: **positional bias penalizes relevant and distracting passages equally**. In real retrieval, over 60% of queries contain at least one hard distractor in the top-10 results. Moving a relevant passage to a high-attention position simultaneously moves a distractor there too, roughly canceling the benefit. This finding does not invalidate the Lost in the Middle effect — Qwen 2.5 7B still shows a **~10 percentage point** swing between middle and edge positions in controlled settings — but it substantially tempers expectations for production reordering gains.

A separate line of work tackles the problem differently. [Jin et al. (ICLR 2025)](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2410.05983) propose a **sandwich interleaving pattern** that places odd-ranked passages at the beginning and even-ranked passages at the end, exploiting U-shaped attention without simply inverting retriever order. They also document that increasing retrieved passages does not consistently improve performance — accuracy initially rises then declines, especially with stronger retrievers that produce harder negatives. For medical RAG, [a 2025 Nature study](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-025-01651-w) proposes a map-reduce "BriefContext" strategy that sidesteps the problem entirely by splitting long-context reasoning into short-context subtasks, achieving **92.61% recall** at predicting when lost-in-the-middle degradation will occur.

Anthropic's own evaluation work provides important context. When [Gregory Kamradt's needle-in-a-haystack test](https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-2-1-prompting) initially showed Claude 2.1 achieving only **27% retrieval accuracy** across 200K tokens, Anthropic demonstrated that a single prompt addition — "Here is the most relevant sentence in the context:" — raised accuracy to **98%**. By [Claude 3 Opus](https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-3-family), needle-in-a-haystack recall exceeded **99%** across the full context window, with the model sometimes identifying that the needle was artificially inserted. These results suggest that position bias is not fixed architecture-level limitation but an evolving property that model training can substantially mitigate.

## How LlamaIndex and LangChain actually assemble context

Production RAG frameworks have developed distinct approaches to the context assembly problem. **LlamaIndex** defaults to a `compact` response mode that concatenates retrieved chunks until they fill the available context window, then overflows into an iterative `refine` cycle. The framework [automatically calculates token budgets](https://docs.llamaindex.ai/en/stable/module_guides/querying/response_synthesizers/) by measuring prompt template size and subtracting from the LLM's declared context window. When overflow occurs, a `TokenTextSplitter` with configurable overlap breaks the packed context into segments, each processed sequentially with a refine template that carries forward the previous answer.

LlamaIndex offers [five primary synthesis modes](https://docs.llamaindex.ai/en/stable/module_guides/querying/response_synthesizers/). The default **`compact`** minimizes LLM calls while handling overflow gracefully. **`refine`** processes one chunk per LLM call with an optional `structured_answer_filtering` flag that uses function calling to skip irrelevant nodes. **`tree_summarize`** queries packed groups independently, then recursively merges answers until one remains — parallelizable via `use_async=True` and producing more concise outputs because hierarchical summarization compresses at each stage. **`accumulate`** runs the query against each chunk independently, useful for multi-perspective synthesis, while **`simple_summarize`** truncates everything to fit a single call, trading completeness for speed.

Before synthesis, LlamaIndex's [node postprocessors](https://docs.llamaindex.ai/en/stable/module_guides/querying/node_postprocessors/node_postprocessors/) handle filtering and reordering. The `SimilarityPostprocessor` applies a hard similarity cutoff (e.g., 0.7). The `SentenceEmbeddingOptimizer` removes irrelevant sentences *within* chunks using embedding similarity, directly reducing token waste. For deduplication, the `EmbeddingRecencyPostprocessor` removes older nodes that are semantically too similar to newer ones. A `LongInTheMiddleReorder` postprocessor implements the sandwich pattern from lost-in-the-middle research.

**LangChain** takes a more modular approach. The [`StuffDocumentsChain`](https://api.python.langchain.com/en/latest/chains/langchain.chains.combine_documents.stuff.StuffDocumentsChain.html) (now `create_stuff_documents_chain`) joins documents with a configurable separator (default `"\n\n"`) and inserts them into a prompt variable, but performs **no automatic overflow handling** — if documents exceed the context window, the call fails. The [`MapReduceDocumentsChain`](https://api.python.langchain.com/en/latest/chains/langchain.chains.combine_documents.map_reduce.MapReduceDocumentsChain.html) applies an LLM to each document independently (parallelizable), then reduces all outputs through a `ReduceDocumentsChain` with a configurable `token_max` parameter (e.g., 4000 tokens per group). `RefineDocumentsChain` loops sequentially, like LlamaIndex's refine mode.

LangChain's most distinctive context assembly feature is the [`LongContextReorder`](https://python.langchain.com/v0.2/docs/how_to/long_context_reorder/) transformer, which directly implements the Lost in the Middle findings. The algorithm reverses the document list, then alternates appending to the end and inserting at the beginning, producing a pattern where the most relevant documents occupy the first and last positions. For deduplication, the [`EmbeddingsRedundantFilter`](https://python.langchain.com/v0.2/docs/how_to/contextual_compression/) removes near-duplicate documents based on inter-document embedding similarity, while the `EmbeddingsFilter` applies a similarity threshold (e.g., 0.76) for relevance filtering. These can be chained in a `DocumentCompressorPipeline` — LangChain reports **~4.5x compression** from the `EmbeddingsFilter` alone (3565→787 characters), and **~3x compression** from `LLMChainExtractor`.

A key architectural difference: LlamaIndex carries retrieval scores as first-class `NodeWithScore` objects throughout the pipeline, enabling score-based filtering at every stage. LangChain uses `Document` metadata dictionaries, requiring explicit extraction for score-based operations.

## What metadata costs and what it buys

The token overhead of including metadata in context windows is modest but non-trivial. [Anthropic's Contextual Retrieval approach](https://www.anthropic.com/news/contextual-retrieval) prepends **50–100 tokens** of chunk-specific context (document summary, section heading, positional information) to each chunk before embedding. With a typical retrieval window of 10–20 chunks, this adds **500–2,000 tokens** of metadata. In a standard RAG prompt of 4,000–8,000 context tokens, metadata represents roughly **2–7% of total context budget** — a manageable cost given the returns.

Those returns are substantial at the retrieval stage. Anthropic's experiments show Contextual Embeddings reduced top-20-chunk retrieval failure rate by **35%** (5.7%→3.7%). Combining Contextual Embeddings with Contextual BM25 achieved a **49% reduction** (5.7%→2.9%), and adding a reranker pushed the reduction to **67%** (5.7%→1.9%). [Unstructured.io independently validated](https://unstructured.io/blog/contextual-chunking-in-unstructured-platform-boost-your-rag-retrieval-accuracy) these results on SEC filings, achieving up to **84% reduction** in retrieval failure with contextual chunking.

For citation accuracy specifically, metadata is nearly essential. [Production implementations](https://particula.tech/blog/fix-rag-citations) format context as `Context from [Q3_Financial_Report.pdf, Section: Revenue Analysis, Page: 12]: 'Revenue increased 23%...'` to maintain source awareness during generation. Without such metadata, baseline LLM citation accuracy hovers around [**74%**](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.15629v2) for popular generative search engines. However, [Tensorlake's analysis](https://www.tensorlake.ai/blog/rag-citations) warns that excessive metadata can "pollute" chunk content, recommending that spatial and structural metadata be stored alongside vectors but selectively injected into prompts.

## Token budgets, saturation points, and compression frontiers

Empirical evidence converges on clear token budget allocation patterns. [Production frameworks recommend](https://www.getmaxim.ai/articles/context-engineering-for-ai-agents-production-optimization-strategies/) allocating **30–40%** of the context window to knowledge context, **10–15%** to system instructions, **20–30%** to conversation history, and **10–15%** as a buffer reserve, with an optimal context utilization rate of **60–80%**. Beyond this range, [Chroma's research](https://www.getmaxim.ai/articles/context-engineering-for-ai-agents-production-optimization-strategies/) shows accuracy can degrade from **98.1% to 64.1%** based purely on context structuring.

The question of how many chunks to include has a nuanced answer. [Databricks' long-context RAG study](https://www.databricks.com/blog/long-context-rag-performance-llms) found saturation points of **16K tokens for GPT-4-turbo and Claude-3-Sonnet**, **4K for Mixtral-instruct**, and **8K for DBRX-instruct**, though newer models like GPT-4o and Claude-3.5-Sonnet show minimal degradation as context grows. An [arXiv study on context window utilization](https://arxiv.org/html/2407.19794v2) found that filling the entire window showed **no notable enhancement** in similarity scores — smaller chunks with less utilization led to better outcomes. The BABILong benchmark suggests LLMs effectively utilize only **10–20% of context**, with performance declining sharply as reasoning complexity increases.

The emerging consensus is to **retrieve generously (top 10–20 chunks) but generate with 3–5** after aggressive reranking and filtering. Anthropic found [**20 chunks optimal for retrieval**](https://www.anthropic.com/news/contextual-retrieval), testing against k=5 and k=10.

For pipelines where context length remains a bottleneck, compression techniques offer remarkable efficiency gains. [LLMLingua (Microsoft, EMNLP 2023)](https://llmlingua.com/) achieves up to **20x compression** with only **1.5% performance loss** by using a small language model to score and remove low-information tokens. Its RAG-specific successor, [LongLLMLingua](https://www.llamaindex.ai/blog/longllmlingua-bye-bye-to-middle-loss-and-save-on-your-rag-costs-via-prompt-compression-54b559b9ddf7), improves RAG performance by **21.4% using only one-quarter of the tokens** and explicitly mitigates lost-in-the-middle effects. [ACC-RAG (EMNLP 2025)](https://aclanthology.org/2025.findings-emnlp.1307/) takes this further with adaptive compression rates based on input complexity, achieving **over 4x faster inference** while maintaining accuracy — addressing the fundamental limitation of fixed-rate compression, which over-compresses simple queries and under-compresses complex ones.

## Conclusion

Three findings reshape how practitioners should think about context window packing. First, **position bias is real but self-limiting in production RAG** — the same U-shaped attention curve that penalizes relevant middle-positioned chunks also penalizes distractors, making sophisticated reordering yield only marginal gains over random shuffling with real retrievers. The sandwich pattern remains a low-cost hedge worth implementing, but it is not the lever most practitioners assume. Second, **the retrieve-many-generate-few pattern dominates**: retrieving 20 chunks for recall, then compressing to 3–5 for generation via reranking and filtering, consistently outperforms simply stuffing more passages into larger windows. Third, **metadata pays for itself** — at 2–7% of context budget, chunk-level metadata enables 35–67% retrieval failure reduction and meaningfully improves citation grounding, making it one of the highest-ROI investments in the RAG pipeline. The real frontier is not bigger context windows but smarter context assembly: adaptive compression, query-dependent chunk selection, and dynamic budget allocation that treats the context window as a scarce reasoning resource rather than a passive document holder.
