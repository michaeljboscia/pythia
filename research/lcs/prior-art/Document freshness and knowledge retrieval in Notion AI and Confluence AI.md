# Document freshness and knowledge retrieval in Notion AI and Confluence AI

**Notion AI and Confluence AI take fundamentally different architectural approaches to the same hard problem—keeping AI responses grounded in living documents—and both fall short on semantic freshness detection, cross-document contradiction awareness, and permission enforcement at the agent layer.** This matters because enterprises adopting these systems for internal knowledge retrieval are discovering that RAG-powered AI confidently surfaces stale information with no warning to the user. The gap between what these systems promise (always-current, permission-aware knowledge retrieval) and what they deliver (timestamp-boosted vector search with known leakage vectors) offers concrete lessons for anyone building a developer-focused knowledge system. This analysis grounds every claim in published sources to serve as a reference architecture comparison.

## Two architectures, one retrieval problem

Notion AI and Confluence AI (powered by Atlassian Rovo) represent two distinct design philosophies for enterprise knowledge retrieval. Notion builds on a **flat vector search model**—content is chunked into spans, embedded, and stored in Turbopuffer with permission metadata attached per span. Confluence AI builds on the **Teamwork Graph**, a semantic knowledge graph that maps relationships between people, teams, projects, content, and decisions across 100+ connected applications.

Notion's retrieval pipeline follows a [documented six-step process](https://www.notion.com/help/notion-ai-security-practices): a user query hits the LLM, which determines whether workspace search is needed; if so, it generates a search query, passes it to Turbopuffer for semantic similarity matching, then uses the LLM again to rank and synthesize relevant pages into a response. The system uses models from both [Anthropic (Claude) and OpenAI (GPT)](https://www.notion.com/help/notion-ai-security-practices), with Enterprise plans receiving zero-retention API calls. Notion's [engineering blog](https://www.notion.com/blog/two-years-of-vector-search-at-notion) reveals the vector infrastructure scaled from launch in November 2023 to **multi-billion objects** in Turbopuffer by 2026, with p50 latency of **50–70ms** after migration from a previous provider.

Rovo's architecture is substantially more complex. The [Atlassian engineering team published](https://www.atlassian.com/blog/atlassian-engineering/unraveling-rovo-search) a detailed breakdown of a **multi-layer ranking pipeline**: L1 retrieval uses BM25 lexical matching plus KNN vector search in AWS OpenSearch with permission-check clauses; L2 reranks using a fine-tuned cross-encoder model for semantic relevance and a Deep & Cross Network for behavioral signals, hosted on AWS SageMaker; L3 interleaves results across products based on product affinity. A [separate entity linking service](https://www.atlassian.com/blog/atlassian-engineering/how-rovo-solves-search-challenges-entity-linking) launched in January 2026 uses a cascade architecture—transformer-based detection, three parallel recall paths (collaboration-based, lexical, and embedding-based), and XLM-RoBERTa cross-encoder ranking—to connect mentions in text to Teamwork Graph entries. Rovo supports search across **50+ SaaS applications** through three connector types: full content ingestion, linked content ingestion, and [federated search](https://www.atlassian.com/blog/atlassian-engineering/unraveling-rovo-search).

The architectural divergence is significant. Notion treats documents as independent objects in embedding space. Rovo treats them as nodes in a relationship graph. This difference shapes every downstream behavior—from freshness handling to cross-document awareness.

## How each system handles document freshness

Neither system has solved semantic freshness—the ability to determine whether content is *factually current* rather than merely *recently edited*. Both rely on temporal proxies, but their implementations differ meaningfully.

**Notion's dual-path indexing** is its freshness foundation. The [February 2026 engineering blog post](https://www.notion.com/blog/two-years-of-vector-search-at-notion) describes two paths: an **offline batch pipeline** using Apache Spark for bulk embedding generation, and an **online real-time pipeline** using Kafka consumers that process page edits with **sub-minute latency**. Notion uses [Confluent Cloud (managed Kafka)](https://www.confluent.io/customers/notion/) as the backbone, where "changes made within the application are instantly reflected in its vector database for RAG." A July 2025 optimization introduced **64-bit xxHash comparison** per span—when a page is edited, only spans where the text hash changed get re-embedded, achieving a **70% reduction in data volume** for both embedding API calls and vector DB writes. The underlying [data lake](https://www.notion.com/blog/building-and-scaling-notions-data-lake) reduced end-to-end ingestion latency from over a day to minutes for small tables.

However, **no public documentation confirms that Notion AI uses freshness signals in retrieval ranking**. The vector search returns results by semantic similarity; there is no documented recency boost, edit-frequency weighting, or view-count influence. Deleted pages have their embeddings removed [within 60 days](https://www.notion.com/help/notion-ai-security-practices), with deleted connector content becoming "immediately unsearchable." For [Enterprise Search connectors](https://www.notion.com/help/enterprise-search-security-and-privacy-practices), deleted content takes 30 minutes to one hour to become unsearchable. AI responses include source page citations but **no timestamps or freshness indicators** alongside those citations.

**Confluence AI/Rovo explicitly incorporates freshness into ranking.** The [Atlassian engineering blog](https://www.atlassian.com/blog/atlassian-engineering/unraveling-rovo-search) states: "Freshness is determined by the latest updated time and is treated differently across various products in months, weeks or days. We boost fresher results to the top and penalize older results." The system also factors in **container type, content length, number of contributors, and activity signals** (views, likes, comments) to determine content authority. Webhook-driven synchronization keeps the index current, and Rovo [listens for permission changes and content deletion](https://support.atlassian.com/rovo/docs/rovo-data-privacy-and-usage-guidelines/) to update the index in near-real-time.

But both systems share the same fundamental blind spot. As [one detailed analysis](https://cotera.co/articles/confluence-ai-features-guide) documented: "There's no stale content detection whatsoever. Confluence tracks edit dates, but that metric is almost useless for freshness. A page touched two weeks ago might reference a deprecated API that nobody caught. Meanwhile, a page untouched for six months might be perfectly accurate." This semantic freshness gap means a recently-edited page containing outdated technical references will rank *higher* than an untouched but accurate page in Rovo's system. Notion's system, lacking even timestamp-based boosting in its documented pipeline, treats both pages equivalently based on embedding similarity alone.

Research from the academic community confirms this is a hard, unsolved problem. An [ArXiv paper on temporal RAG](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.19376) found that a simple recency prior achieved 1.00 accuracy on freshness tasks, but clustering heuristics for topic evolution (detecting when content has become semantically stale) scored just **0.08 F1**. The implication: timestamp-based freshness is easy; semantic freshness detection remains an open research problem.

## Cross-document relationships and the graph advantage

When Document A references Document B and B is updated, the handling differs sharply between the two systems—and **neither fully solves the cascading update problem**.

Notion supports [backlinks](https://www.notion.com/help/create-links-and-backlinks) (bi-directional, auto-generated when pages are @-mentioned or linked), [synced blocks](https://www.notion.com/help/synced-blocks) (content appearing in multiple pages that updates everywhere when edited in one location), and [database relations and rollups](https://www.notion.com/help/relations-and-rollups). These are structural features of the product, not the AI layer. No published documentation indicates that **Notion AI traverses backlinks, follows relations, or resolves synced blocks during retrieval**. The documented pipeline is flat: LLM generates search query → Turbopuffer returns semantically similar pages → LLM ranks and synthesizes. If Document B is updated, Notion's online indexing re-embeds B's changed spans within sub-minutes. But Document A's embedding **remains unchanged** unless A itself is edited—meaning the AI may retrieve A's now-stale reference to B's old content without awareness that B has been updated.

Confluence AI, through the [Teamwork Graph](https://developer.atlassian.com/platform/teamwork-graph/what-is-teamwork-graph/), takes a structurally different approach. The graph maps three types of relationships: canonical (structural connections like Epic → Story), activity (behavioral data like User → viewed → Page), and inferred (ML-derived connections like collaboration patterns). Every item across Atlassian products and 100+ connected applications is represented as a standardized object with defined properties and relationship edges. The [Rovo Deep Research](https://www.atlassian.com/blog/atlassian-engineering/how-rovo-deep-research-works) feature performs multi-hop graph traversal—decomposing complex queries into sub-tasks, retrieving across multiple paths concurrently, and synthesizing results into structured reports with citations. This can trace chains like service → ticket → code commit to build factual answers.

Yet even the Teamwork Graph **does not propagate cascading awareness**. When Document B is updated, the graph updates B's node. But there is no documented mechanism for flagging that Document A (which references B) should be re-evaluated. The relationship edge exists, but the system does not use it to trigger re-indexing or freshness warnings on dependent documents. More critically, [neither system detects cross-page contradictions](https://cotera.co/articles/confluence-ai-features-guide): "Confluence AI will happily summarize each one in isolation. It won't notice they disagree with each other, won't tell you which is authoritative, and definitely won't suggest merging or archiving the stale one."

The Teamwork Graph does provide a meaningful advantage for *discovery*. Rovo can surface related content across tools that a user might not have found through keyword or vector search alone—connecting a Confluence design document to Jira implementation tickets to Slack discussion threads. Notion's flat vector approach lacks this structural awareness, relying entirely on semantic similarity in embedding space to surface related content.

## Three anti-patterns emerging in production

### Stale summaries without user-visible decay signals

Both systems generate AI responses without indicating the age or reliability of underlying sources. Notion AI Q&A [provides source page citations](https://www.notion.com/blog/introducing-q-and-a) but no timestamps. Rovo includes source attribution but explicitly [warns users](https://support.atlassian.com/rovo/docs/using-rovo-chat/) not to rely on it "in cases where you need current and accurate information about people, places, and facts." A [practitioner review of Notion AI](https://www.simonesmerilli.com/life/notion-ai-qa) found it "sometimes hallucinates on questions that can be answered with content present in your Notion workspace," particularly for database queries.

The deeper problem is what one [RAG practitioner calls](https://glenrhodes.com/data-freshness-rot-as-the-silent-failure-mode-in-production-rag-systems-and-treating-document-shelf-life-as-a-first-class-reliability-concern-3/) "data freshness rot": "A document from eighteen months ago that closely matches a user query will score just as high in cosine similarity as a document from yesterday. The retriever does not know the document is stale. The model does not know. And the user certainly does not know." A [VentureBeat analysis](https://venturebeat.com/orchestration/enterprises-are-measuring-the-wrong-part-of-rag) found that "silent drift accumulates upstream, and by the time issues surface, failures are often misattributed to model behavior rather than the retrieval system itself." One enterprise case study reported [12% of documents exceeding acceptable staleness thresholds](https://ragaboutit.com/the-knowledge-decay-problem-how-to-build-rag-systems-that-stay-fresh-at-scale/), with maximum staleness reaching **45 days** against a 7-day target.

### Citation drift and provenance fragility

Industry-wide, [citation accuracy rates are below 66%](https://www.ekamoira.com/blog/ai-citations-llm-sources) across major AI platforms. The [Confluence AI accuracy](https://cotera.co/articles/confluence-ai-features-guide) in one evaluation was "right about 85% of the time—the misses were mostly pages with gnarly tables or conditional logic." When content changes, citation stability depends on implementation details. Notion uses [content-hash-based span tracking](https://www.notion.com/blog/two-years-of-vector-search-at-notion) (xxHash per span) that survives re-indexing—a strong technical foundation. Rovo's passage-level citations [inject page-level metadata](https://www.atlassian.com/blog/atlassian-engineering/unraveling-rovo-search) (URL, title) into each chunk during processing, tying citations to the source document rather than specific content positions.

The broader challenge, as [one architecture analysis](https://www.buzzi.ai/insights/ai-document-retrieval-rag-citation-architecture) notes: "Reindexing changes IDs, documents move, headings are edited, and links rot." Best practice is "chunk IDs that survive reindexing—content hashing plus document versioning." Notion's xxHash approach aligns with this recommendation; Atlassian's approach is less documented at the chunk-ID level.

### Permission leakage at the agent boundary

Both systems claim rigorous permission enforcement for basic search and chat. Notion states that [AI honors existing permissions](https://www.notion.com/help/notion-ai-security-practices) and stores permissions as metadata on every vector span. Rovo performs [dual-layer permission checks](https://www.atlassian.com/blog/atlassian-engineering/unraveling-rovo-search)—at L1 retrieval and post-L2 reranking with the source product.

But **agents break the permission model** in both systems. Notion's [Custom Agents operate with their own independent permissions](https://www.notion.com/help/custom-agents-security-features) separate from individual users, meaning someone using an agent might access information they couldn't access directly. In September 2025, [security researchers demonstrated](https://the-decoder.com/notion-ai-agents-get-security-update-after-data-leak/) that a malicious PDF could trick a Notion AI agent into uploading private client data to an attacker's server via indirect prompt injection—exploiting what they called the "lethal trifecta" of LLM agents, tool access, and long-term memory. Notion acknowledged the vulnerability and [deployed upgraded detection systems](https://www.notion.com/help/how-notion-protects-against-prompt-injection-risks).

Rovo has a parallel issue. An [Atlassian Community report](https://community.atlassian.com/forums/Confluence-questions/About-using-Rovo-Agents/qaq-p/3086460) documented that "the agent can still share text-based information even when the user isn't allowed to access the original content" and that "users can get information from restricted Confluence spaces by simply asking other Rovo agents." The official position remains that [Rovo respects permissions](https://support.atlassian.com/rovo/docs/rovo-data-privacy-and-usage-guidelines/), but the agent layer introduces a new attack surface that basic query-time permission checks were not designed to handle.

This pattern is not unique to these systems. Microsoft Copilot experienced [similar over-permissioning issues at scale](https://www.metomic.io/resource-centre/what-are-the-security-risks-of-microsoft-co-pilot), with over **15% of business-critical files at risk** from oversharing and a bug that [bypassed Data Loss Prevention policies](https://www.foxnews.com/tech/why-microsoft-365-copilot-bug-matters-data-security) to summarize confidential emails.

## What transfers to a developer-focused knowledge system

The failures of both Notion AI and Confluence AI point to specific architectural requirements for any system that must keep AI responses grounded in living documentation.

**Freshness needs to be a first-class retrieval signal, not just an indexing optimization.** Notion's sub-minute indexing and Rovo's timestamp-based boosting address different parts of the freshness problem but neither addresses semantic staleness. A developer knowledge system should implement **document TTLs by content class**—API references and release notes with 2–4 week review windows, architecture documents with 6-month cycles—and surface staleness confidence alongside every AI response. The [ArXiv research on temporal RAG](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.19376) demonstrates that a fused score combining semantic similarity with a half-life recency prior is both lightweight and effective.

**Cross-document dependency tracking requires explicit graph structure.** Notion's flat vector approach cannot detect when an upstream document change invalidates a downstream reference. Rovo's Teamwork Graph provides the right structural foundation but does not yet use relationship edges for cascading freshness propagation. A developer system should maintain an explicit dependency graph where document updates trigger freshness re-evaluation of all dependent documents—not re-embedding, but flagging for potential staleness. As [Forrester notes](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/the-graphic-future-of-it-management/): "AI requires structured knowledge. Graphs provide a foundational knowledge model that enhances AI-driven automation, reasoning, and prediction."

**Permission enforcement must extend to the agent layer from day one.** Both Notion and Rovo retroactively discovered that agents—which hold their own credentials and access scopes—create permission boundaries that query-time checks cannot enforce. Building a developer knowledge system with AI agents means designing permission models that treat agent actions as user-equivalent operations, with the same access controls applied to agent-retrieved context as to direct user queries. The [Notion approach of granting agents independent permissions](https://www.notion.com/help/custom-agents-security-features) is architecturally convenient but creates a permission elevation vector.

**Citation architecture should use content-addressed chunk IDs.** Notion's xxHash-per-span approach—storing text hash plus metadata hash in DynamoDB—provides stable references that survive re-indexing. This is materially better than position-based or index-ID-based citation, which breaks when content is restructured. A developer system should adopt content-addressed identifiers (combining document version, chunk position, and content hash) as the canonical citation reference.

**Contradiction detection across documents is the unsolved frontier.** Neither system attempts it. Both will confidently return answers synthesized from contradictory sources without flagging the inconsistency. For developer documentation—where conflicting API specifications or deployment procedures cause real production incidents—this is not an acceptable gap. Implementing even basic contradiction detection (comparing retrieved chunks for factual consistency before synthesis) would represent a meaningful advance over both systems.

## Conclusion

Notion AI and Confluence AI represent the current state of the art in enterprise knowledge retrieval from living documents—and the state of the art has significant gaps. Notion excels at indexing speed (sub-minute latency, efficient hash-based change detection) but lacks freshness-aware ranking and cross-document relationship traversal. Confluence AI, through Rovo and the Teamwork Graph, provides richer structural awareness and explicit freshness boosting but cannot detect semantic staleness or cross-page contradictions. Both systems have permission models that hold for basic search but break at the agent boundary, where indirect prompt injection and independent agent credentials create documented data leakage vectors.

The key insight for building a developer-focused knowledge system is that **the retrieval layer, not the generation layer, determines trustworthiness**. Notion and Confluence both use frontier LLMs. Their failures are almost entirely retrieval failures—stale content ranked highly, contradictory sources synthesized without warning, restricted content surfaced through agent-layer permission gaps. Treating document freshness, dependency tracking, and permission enforcement as first-class concerns in the retrieval pipeline—rather than relying on the LLM to compensate—is the primary lesson both systems teach through their limitations.
