# Graph traversal algorithms for knowledge-graph-augmented RAG

**Personalized PageRank dominates subgraph extraction for RAG systems, but at a steep computational price.** The LEGO-GraphRAG benchmark (VLDB 2025) shows PPR consistently delivers the highest recall and F1 across knowledge graph QA tasks, yet takes ~70 seconds per query on Freebase's 100M-node graph — making it the primary runtime bottleneck. Meanwhile, Microsoft's GraphRAG demonstrates that community-based traversal via Leiden clustering can match or exceed naive RAG comprehensiveness by **72–83%** while consuming as little as **2% of the tokens** required by source-text summarization. For engineers choosing between graph databases, Kuzu's embedded architecture with factorized execution delivers **5–188× lower latency** than Neo4j on multi-hop traversals at the 100K-node scale, though Neo4j's ecosystem maturity and GDS library remain the production default for most teams.

This analysis compares three families of traversal strategies — BFS/DFS, Personalized PageRank, and community-based — across retrieval quality, latency, memory, and scalability, grounded in benchmarks from peer-reviewed papers and database documentation.

## PPR outperforms BFS and random walks but costs 700× more time

The most rigorous head-to-head comparison of subgraph extraction strategies comes from **LEGO-GraphRAG**, a modular framework published at VLDB 2025 that decomposes graph-based RAG into Subgraph-Extraction (SE) and Path-Retrieval (PR) stages, then benchmarks combinations across WebQSP, CWQ, GrailQA, and MetaQA datasets on Freebase (~100M nodes, ~300M edges). Three structure-based extraction methods were compared: k-hop Subgraph Extraction (KSE, essentially BFS from seed entities), Random Walk (RW), and Personalized PageRank (PPR). The results are unambiguous: **PPR consistently achieved the highest F1 and Recall** across all subgraph sizes tested. Random Walk struggled to reach 60% recall, making it "nearly unusable in GraphRAG" per the authors. KSE (BFS-style) performed moderately but offered no mechanism for query-aware weighting — it retrieves all k-hop neighbors indiscriminately, causing exponential subgraph growth in dense knowledge graphs with power-law degree distributions (https://www.vldb.org/pvldb/vol18/p3269-cao.pdf).

The computational cost gap is dramatic. On Freebase, PPR-based subgraph extraction required **~70 seconds per query**, while the subsequent path-retrieval step took under 0.1 seconds. Subgraph extraction is the clear bottleneck. BFS-style KSE runs in O(V + E) for the explored subgraph and completes in milliseconds for 2–3 hop expansions, but produces bloated, noisy subgraphs. The standard algorithmic complexities tell the story concisely: BFS and DFS both run in **O(V + E)** time with **O(V)** space, while PPR via power iteration requires **O(k × (V + E))** where k is typically 20–50 iterations for convergence at the standard damping factor of 0.85 (https://networkx.org/documentation/stable/reference/algorithms/generated/networkx.algorithms.link_analysis.pagerank_alg.pagerank.html).

This cost-quality tradeoff has driven most production systems toward a **hybrid pattern**: first extract a coarse 2–4 hop BFS subgraph from seed entities, then apply PPR within that bounded subgraph to distill query-relevant nodes. Systems like RoG, GSR, and GCR all follow this two-stage design (https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.08921). HippoRAG, published at NeurIPS 2024, demonstrated the power of PPR-first retrieval by achieving **up to 20% improvement** over state-of-the-art RAG methods on multi-hop QA benchmarks (MuSiQue, 2WikiMultiHopQA, HotpotQA), while being **10–30× cheaper** and **6–13× faster** than iterative retrieval methods like IRCoT (https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.14831). Its successor, HippoRAG 2, pushed MuSiQue F1 from 44.8 to 51.9 and 2Wiki Recall@5 from 76.5% to 90.4% using a dual-node knowledge graph with passage and phrase nodes (https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.14802).

For BFS versus DFS specifically in RAG contexts, the SG-RAG MOT study tested both orderings for triplet presentation to LLMs on MetaQA-KG. The finding: **no statistically significant difference** between BFS and DFS orderings, though both outperformed random ordering. The authors noted DFS is "more susceptible to issues such as potential loops and needing stronger reasoning capabilities to handle backtracking" (https://www.mdpi.com/2504-4990/7/3/74). In practice, BFS is preferred because its layer-by-layer expansion parallels GNN message-passing — the SEPTA system showed that BFS sampling retains "almost no information loss" compared to full-graph GNN representations (https://arxiv.org/html/2411.06866v1).

For engineers operating at scale, approximate PPR algorithms offer a critical escape from the 70-second bottleneck. **FAST-PPR** (bidirectional PPR) achieves sub-0.2-second query times on Twitter's 1.5-billion-edge graph by combining forward push with Monte Carlo random walks. PPRGo processes graphs with 12M+ nodes in under 2 minutes including preprocessing (https://arxiv.org/html/2403.05198v1). NetworkX's pure-Python PPR implementation should be avoided for anything beyond prototyping: on the Pokec social network (1.6M nodes, 30.6M edges), NetworkX PageRank took **611 seconds** versus networkit's **0.2 seconds** — a 3,000× gap (https://www.timlrx.com/blog/benchmark-of-popular-graph-network-packages/).

## GraphRAG's community summaries dominate on global sensemaking queries

Microsoft's GraphRAG paper, "From Local to Global: A Graph RAG Approach to Query-Focused Summarization" by Darren Edge, Ha Trinh, Newman Cheng, Joshua Bradley, Alex Chao, Apurva Mody, Steven Truitt, Dasha Metropolitansky, Robert Osazuwa Ness, and Jonathan Larson, introduces a fundamentally different retrieval paradigm: rather than traversing multi-hop paths from seed entities, GraphRAG partitions the entire knowledge graph into hierarchical communities using the **Leiden algorithm**, generates LLM-authored summaries for each community, then answers queries via map-reduce over those summaries (https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.16130).

The Leiden algorithm is applied recursively to produce a hierarchy of communities (C0 through C3, from coarsest to finest granularity) that form a **mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive** partition of the graph. Edge weights represent duplicate relationship counts from extraction. On their podcast dataset (8,564 nodes, 20,691 edges) and news dataset (15,754 nodes, 19,520 edges), this hierarchy enabled global question-answering that naive vector-search RAG fundamentally cannot perform.

The benchmark results are striking. Against naive semantic-search RAG (SS), GraphRAG achieved **comprehensiveness win rates of 72–83%** (p<.001) and **diversity win rates of 62–82%** (p<.01) across both datasets and all community levels. Even the coarsest level (C0, root communities) maintained a 72% comprehensiveness win rate while consuming only **2% of the token budget** — just 26,657 tokens for the podcast dataset versus 1,014,611 for full source-text summarization. The finest community level (C3) required 26–33% fewer tokens than source-text summarization while delivering the highest diversity scores (https://arxiv.org/html/2404.16130v2).

The key architectural insight is that community-based retrieval solves a problem multi-hop traversal cannot: **global sensemaking queries** that require synthesizing themes across an entire corpus. Questions like "What are the main themes in this dataset?" have no single seed entity to traverse from. GraphRAG's map-reduce pipeline handles this by processing community summaries in parallel (map phase), scoring each intermediate answer for helpfulness (0–100), filtering zero-score answers, then aggregating the best intermediate answers into a final response (reduce phase). Random shuffling of community summaries across map-phase chunks prevents concentration of relevant information in a single context window.

The trade-off is clear: **naive RAG won on directness** (concision) across all comparisons, which the authors deliberately included as a control metric. Community summarization produces richer, more comprehensive answers at the cost of directness — a sensible trade-off for analytical queries but potentially undesirable for simple factoid retrieval. The claim-based validation (Experiment 2) confirmed these findings: all community-level conditions produced statistically significantly more claims and more diverse claim clusters than semantic search.

The indexing cost is non-trivial. The paper used GPT-4 Turbo with 600-token extraction windows and found that smaller chunks extracted **nearly twice as many entity references** as 2,400-token chunks on HotPotQA. The GitHub repository explicitly warns that "GraphRAG indexing can be an expensive operation" (https://github.com/microsoft/graphrag). For the implementation, community detection uses the graspologic library, and the system now also supports local search (entity neighborhood fan-out), DRIFT search (entity-focused with community context), and basic vector search modes.

## Multi-hop traversal latency: Kuzu's factorized execution changes the game

Property graph database choice dramatically affects multi-hop traversal performance. The most detailed Kuzu-versus-Neo4j comparison used a social network with **100K nodes and ~2.4M edges** on a MacBook Pro, testing both via Python clients. Simple multi-hop queries (persons by country, requiring 2-hop traversal through city nodes) showed Kuzu completing in **7.8 milliseconds** versus Neo4j's 47.3 milliseconds — a 6.1× speedup. But the gap explodes for path-heavy queries: counting all second-degree connection paths and filtering multi-hop paths showed Kuzu achieving **70–188× speedups** over Neo4j, attributable to Kuzu's **factorized execution** and worst-case optimal join (WCOJ) algorithms that avoid materializing exponentially large intermediate results (https://thedataquarry.com/blog/embedded-db-2/).

At smaller scale (800 nodes on Neo4j AuraDB Free), multi-hop latency scales modestly: **29ms for 1-hop, 37ms for 2-hop, and 48ms for 3-hop** queries against a friend-of-friend social network (https://medium.com/@confusedcyberwarrior/graph-databases-are-multi-hop-queries-really-slower-90193acf5e49). But the Memgraph benchmark on Pokec (a real social network) revealed Neo4j Community Edition's 4-hop expansion hitting **3.1 seconds** at p99, with 1-hop p99 at 27.96ms and throughput of only 280 queries per second (https://memgraph.com/blog/memgraph-vs-neo4j-performance-benchmark-comparison).

The Kuzu CIDR 2023 paper provides the theoretical foundation for these results. Kuzu's query processor performs **chained sideways information passing (SIP)** that propagates filtering predicates from one hop to non-adjacent nodes, pruning irrelevant intermediate results before they materialize. On LDBC-type queries with ≥2 hops over many-to-many relationships, Kuzu consistently outperformed both DuckDB and Umbra. The system is regularly tested at **LDBC SF100 scale (280M nodes, 1.7B edges)** on a single machine (https://www.cidrdb.org/cidr2023/papers/p48-jin.pdf).

Memory architecture differs fundamentally between the two systems. Neo4j's GDS library operates entirely on the JVM heap, with Neo4j documentation recommending **~90% of available RAM** for purely analytical workloads. The recommended production configuration for a 16GB machine allocates 5GB heap, 7GB page cache, and 4GB for the OS. Variable-length path queries can cause memory overestimation due to shared relationships between result paths (https://neo4j.com/docs/graph-data-science/current/installation/System-requirements/). Kuzu takes a fundamentally different approach: **disk-based columnar storage** with Compressed Sparse Row (CSR) indices for adjacency traversals, using morsel-driven parallelism over vectors of 2,048 tuples. Factorized intermediate representations provide what the authors describe as "exponential reduction in memory" for many-to-many joins — precisely the pattern that dominates multi-hop knowledge graph traversal.

The architectural differences extend beyond raw performance. Neo4j's client-server model adds Bolt protocol serialization overhead on every query; one community report noted "the communication time took the most part of time" when measuring GDS algorithm runtimes via Python. Kuzu's embedded design eliminates this entirely — zero sockets, zero serialization. Data ingestion benchmarks showed Kuzu **~18× faster** than Neo4j via Python clients at the 100K-node scale (https://thedataquarry.com/blog/embedded-db-2/). At LDBC SF-1000 scale (~2.7B nodes), the TigerGraph-versus-Neo4j benchmark showed Neo4j completing only **12 of 25 business intelligence queries** within a 5-hour timeout, with TigerGraph outperforming by 2+ orders of magnitude on many queries (https://arxiv.org/abs/1907.07405).

For RAG system architects operating at the 10K–1M node range typical of domain-specific knowledge graphs, these numbers translate directly: 2-hop traversals complete in **single-digit milliseconds** in Kuzu and **tens of milliseconds** in Neo4j, well within interactive latency budgets. At 3 hops, the gap widens but both remain sub-second for modest graph densities. The critical performance cliff arrives with 4+ hops on dense subgraphs, where Neo4j can breach multi-second latency while Kuzu's factorized execution maintains sub-second performance.

## Conclusion

Three clear engineering recommendations emerge. First, **adopt the hybrid KSE→PPR pattern** for entity-centric queries: extract a bounded 2–3 hop BFS subgraph in milliseconds, then apply approximate PPR within that subgraph for query-aware ranking — this avoids the 70-second full-graph PPR cost while retaining its retrieval quality advantage. Second, **use community-based retrieval (GraphRAG-style) for global analytical queries** where no seed entity exists; the Leiden hierarchy provides 72–83% comprehensiveness gains at 2–33% of the token cost, a trade-off that improves with scale. Third, **prefer embedded graph engines like Kuzu over client-server Neo4j** when the deployment model permits it — the 5–188× latency reduction on multi-hop queries and elimination of serialization overhead directly benefit RAG response times. NetworkX should be confined to prototyping; its 3,000× PageRank slowdown versus C++ alternatives makes it unsuitable for production knowledge graphs exceeding 100K nodes. The field is converging on learned retrieval (GNN-RAG, GFM-RAG) that combines structural traversal with semantic scoring, but PPR-based systems like HippoRAG remain the strongest training-free baseline available today.
