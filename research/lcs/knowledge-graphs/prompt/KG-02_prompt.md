# Research Prompt: KG-02 RAPTOR Paper (Stanford)

## Research Objective
Perform a deep analytical read of the RAPTOR (Recursive Abstractive Processing for Tree-Organized Retrieval) paper from Stanford. Understand how hierarchical indexing—by recursively summarizing and clustering text chunks—solves the problem of retrieving high-level thematic information without losing granular fidelity.

## Research Questions
1. How does the RAPTOR algorithm physically construct its tree? Detail the exact clustering mechanism (e.g., Gaussian Mixture Models, UMAP) applied to the vector embeddings.
2. How does the recursive summarization step work? When multiple leaf nodes are clustered, what is the structure of the prompt used to generate the parent node's summary?
3. During retrieval, what is the difference between RAPTOR's "Tree Traversal" querying and "Collapsed Tree" querying? Which yields better recall on multi-hop QA benchmarks?
4. How does RAPTOR handle updates? If a single source document changes, how much of the tree must be recalculated and re-summarized?
5. What are the measured recall improvements of RAPTOR over baseline RAG on benchmarks like QuALITY or QASper?
6. How does RAPTOR's semantic clustering approach compare to Microsoft's GraphRAG entity-centric approach for corpus-wide synthesis?

## Starting Sources
- **RAPTOR Paper:** "RAPTOR: Recursive Abstractive Processing for Tree-Organized Retrieval" (Sarthi et al., Stanford) - https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.18059
- **LlamaIndex RAPTOR Implementation:** https://docs.llamaindex.ai/en/stable/examples/pack/raptor_pack/

## What to Measure & Compare
- Calculate the storage multiplier effect: How many additional vectors and tokens of text are generated by the parent abstraction nodes compared to just storing the leaf chunks?
- Compare the computational cost of RAPTOR's UMAP+GMM clustering versus GraphRAG's Leiden community detection.

## Definition of Done
A comprehensive evaluation of the RAPTOR methodology. The output must clearly dictate whether hierarchical summarization trees are a viable alternative (or complement) to knowledge graphs for LCS, particularly regarding how they handle dynamically changing source code.

## Architectural Implication
This is a **P0 BLOCKER** for **ADR-004 (Chunking Strategy)**. It determines if LCS will implement a flat chunking strategy or a hierarchical, abstractive tree structure for long-context retrieval.
