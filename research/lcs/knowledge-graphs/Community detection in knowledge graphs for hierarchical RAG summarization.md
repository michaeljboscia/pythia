# Community detection in knowledge graphs for hierarchical RAG summarization

**The Leiden algorithm has become the structural backbone of graph-based Retrieval-Augmented Generation, and for good reason.** Microsoft's [GraphRAG system](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.16130) leverages Leiden community detection to partition knowledge graphs into hierarchical clusters, pre-generate summaries at each level, and then answer global sensemaking queries with **72–83% comprehensiveness win rates** over standard vector RAG. This advantage stems from two compounding innovations: Leiden's mathematical guarantee that every detected community is internally well-connected — a property its predecessor Louvain cannot provide — and a map-reduce summarization pipeline that converts graph structure into layered natural-language abstractions. For knowledge graphs in the 10K–100K node range typical of enterprise RAG deployments, Leiden runs in seconds on commodity hardware, making the LLM calls for entity extraction and summary generation the true cost bottleneck rather than community detection itself.

## Why Leiden replaced Louvain: disconnected communities and the refinement phase

The Louvain algorithm, introduced in 2008, became the default tool for modularity-based community detection due to its speed and simplicity. Its two-phase cycle — local node moves followed by network aggregation — runs in near-linear time. But [Traag, Waltman, and van Eck (2019)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-019-41695-z) demonstrated a critical structural flaw: Louvain can produce **arbitrarily badly connected communities**, including communities that are internally disconnected. The mechanism is straightforward. When a bridge node migrates to a different community, the remaining nodes in the original community may become disconnected. Because each remaining node is still locally optimal, the algorithm never moves them, and after aggregation the disconnected community collapses into a single node that can never be split apart.

The empirical severity is striking. On the Amazon co-purchasing network (334,863 nodes), **up to 25% of communities were badly connected** after four Louvain iterations. On the DBLP co-authorship network (317,080 nodes), up to **16% of communities were outright disconnected**. Iterating Louvain to improve quality actually aggravates the problem, producing nearly tenfold increases in disconnected communities between the first and second iterations on some networks.

Leiden addresses this by inserting a **refinement phase** between local moving and aggregation, creating a three-phase cycle. After the fast local-move phase assigns nodes to communities, the refinement phase starts from a singleton partition within each community and merges nodes back together — but only through well-connected merges. A stochasticity parameter θ (default 0.01) controls how greedily merges are selected, enabling broader exploration of the partition space. Crucially, the aggregate network is built from the refined partition while the initial partition for the next level comes from the unrefined one, giving the algorithm room to split communities that Louvain would permanently fuse.

This architecture yields formal guarantees that [Louvain cannot match](https://www.cwts.nl/blog?article=n-r2u2a4). After each Leiden iteration, all communities are guaranteed to be **γ-connected** (a condition stronger than simple connectivity). After a stable iteration, communities additionally satisfy **subpartition γ-density**, meaning no community can be split into two parts where either part would benefit from separation. Louvain, even after convergence, guarantees only node-level optimality — individual nodes cannot improve by moving — but says nothing about community-level connectivity.

## Modularity and speed: Leiden's quantitative edge

Beyond structural guarantees, Leiden consistently finds **higher-quality partitions faster**. On six empirical networks ranging from 317K to 39.3M nodes, Leiden achieved superior [maximum modularity scores](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-019-41695-z) in every case: **0.8387 vs. 0.8262** on DBLP, **0.9341 vs. 0.9301** on Amazon, **0.7739 vs. 0.7653** on LiveJournal (3.9M nodes), and **0.9801 vs. 0.9796** on the Web UK graph (39.3M nodes). The improvements are modest in absolute terms but consistent, and Leiden continues improving with additional iterations while Louvain quickly plateaus.

The speed differences are more dramatic. On empirical networks, Leiden's first iteration is **2–20× faster** than Louvain's, with the speedup growing for larger graphs. The fast local-move phase uses a queue-based approach that only revisits nodes whose neighborhoods have changed, rather than scanning all nodes each pass. On synthetic benchmarks with difficult community structure, the gap widens further. At 10 million nodes with mixing parameter μ = 0.9 (highly ambiguous community boundaries), [Louvain required approximately 2.5 days while Leiden finished in under 10 minutes](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-019-41695-z) — a roughly **360× speedup**. The theoretical time complexity of both algorithms is **O(L|E|)** where L is the iteration count, but Louvain's constant factor grows substantially for challenging partitions while Leiden's [remains near-linear](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3673038.3673146).

## How GraphRAG converts communities into answerable summaries

Microsoft Research's [GraphRAG pipeline](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.16130) transforms these algorithmic properties into a practical RAG architecture through six indexing steps. Source documents are chunked, then an LLM extracts entities (with types and descriptions) and relationships (source, target, description) from each chunk. Duplicate entities across chunks are merged, and the resulting knowledge graph is partitioned using **hierarchical Leiden community detection** via Microsoft's [graspologic library](https://github.com/graspologic-org/graspologic). The algorithm runs recursively, detecting sub-communities within each community until reaching leaf-level clusters that cannot be further partitioned. This produces a hierarchy where each level provides a mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive partition of all nodes — from broad thematic clusters at the root (C0) down to fine-grained topical groups at the leaves (C3).

The summary generation process operates **bottom-up**. For leaf-level communities, element summaries (entity descriptions, relationship descriptions, associated claims) are ranked by combined source-and-target node degree and packed into an LLM context window until the token limit is reached. The LLM then generates a natural-language summary of that community. For higher-level communities, the system first attempts to fit all element summaries directly; if they exceed the context window, it substitutes sub-community summaries (which are shorter) for their constituent element summaries until everything fits. This produces **pre-computed summaries at every level of the hierarchy** before any query arrives.

At query time, GraphRAG uses a **map-reduce strategy**. Community summaries at a chosen hierarchy level are shuffled and divided into token-limited chunks. Each chunk is independently processed by the LLM to produce an intermediate answer with a self-assessed helpfulness score (0–100). Intermediate answers scoring zero are discarded. The remaining answers are sorted by helpfulness, packed into a final context window, and used to generate the global response. This architecture ensures that every node in the graph contributes to the answer through its community summary — solving the fundamental limitation of vector RAG, which retrieves only top-k similar chunks and systematically misses information distributed across the corpus.

## GraphRAG's measured advantage over standard RAG

The [experimental evaluation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.16130) used two datasets — podcast transcripts and news articles, each approximately one million tokens — with 125 activity-centered sensemaking questions per dataset, evaluated through head-to-head LLM-as-judge comparisons repeated five times each.

On **comprehensiveness** (how thoroughly the answer covers all aspects of the question), GraphRAG achieved win rates of **72–83%** over naive vector RAG on the podcast dataset and **72–80%** on news articles across all four community hierarchy levels, all statistically significant at p < 0.001. On **diversity** (variety of perspectives and information), win rates were **75–82%** for podcasts and **62–71%** for news. Even the root-level communities (C0), which consumed only **2–3% of the token budget** compared to exhaustive text summarization, achieved a 72% comprehensiveness win rate over vector RAG.

A claim-based validation confirmed these results. Answers from all GraphRAG conditions averaged **31–34 extracted claims** per response versus only **25–26 for vector RAG**, a statistically significant gap (p < 0.05) on both datasets. The one metric where vector RAG won was **directness** — its shorter, more focused answers scored higher on specificity, an expected trade-off against comprehensiveness.

The cost profile across hierarchy levels reveals a practical design lever. Root-level summaries (C0) required only **26,657 tokens** per query on the podcast dataset versus **1,014,611 tokens** for exhaustive text summarization — a **97% reduction**. Leaf-level summaries (C3) used 746,100 tokens, still 26% fewer than exhaustive approaches, while achieving the highest quality scores. Practitioners can tune this trade-off by selecting the appropriate community level for their latency and cost constraints.

## Computational costs from 10K to 100K nodes and recomputation strategies

For knowledge graphs in the **10K–100K node range**, Leiden community detection is computationally trivial. Benchmarks from [NVIDIA's cuGraph evaluation](https://developer.nvidia.com/blog/how-to-accelerate-community-detection-in-python-using-gpu-powered-leiden) on a patent citation graph (3.8M nodes, 16.5M edges) show igraph's sequential CPU implementation completing in **~27 seconds**, cuGraph's GPU implementation in **~3 seconds**, and graspologic (used by GraphRAG) in **~145 seconds**. Extrapolating via near-linear O(L|E|) scaling, a 100K-node graph with 500K edges should complete in roughly **1–5 seconds on igraph** and under **200 milliseconds on GPU**. At 10K nodes, runtimes drop to tens of milliseconds. Memory consumption follows **O(|V| + |E|)** — tens to low hundreds of megabytes for graphs in this range, according to [leidenalg documentation](https://github.com/vtraag/leidenalg).

The real bottleneck in GraphRAG is not community detection but LLM inference. The [original paper reports](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.16130) that full graph indexing — entity extraction, community detection, and summary generation — took **281 minutes** for the podcast dataset on a VM with 16 GB RAM. The Leiden step constitutes a tiny fraction of this total. This means that recomputing communities after graph updates is cheap; regenerating community summaries via LLM calls is expensive.

For evolving knowledge graphs, three recomputation strategies exist. **Full recomputation** is practical when the Leiden step takes seconds and summary regeneration can be batched efficiently. **Seeded Leiden**, supported by [Neo4j's Graph Data Science library](https://neo4j.com/docs/graph-data-science/current/algorithms/leiden/), initializes the algorithm with previous community assignments, allowing it to converge faster while preserving stable community identifiers. **Dynamic Frontier approaches**, described in [Sahu (2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.11658), restrict the local-moving phase to vertices affected by recent graph changes while running the refinement phase on all vertices — providing significant speedups when less than 5–10% of the graph changes between snapshots.

In practice, the recomputation trigger should be **change-volume-based rather than time-based**: rerun Leiden after a batch of graph modifications exceeds a threshold percentage of total edges (typically 5–10%), then selectively regenerate summaries only for communities whose membership changed. This incremental strategy avoids the current [GraphRAG limitation](https://learnopencv.com/graphrag-explained-knowledge-graphs-medical/) of reconstructing the entire index on every update.

## Conclusion

Leiden community detection provides GraphRAG with a mathematically grounded partitioning that Louvain's disconnected communities would undermine. The modularity improvements are incremental — typically 0.5–1.5% higher scores — but the structural guarantees and 2–20× speed advantages are decisive for systems that depend on community coherence to generate meaningful summaries. GraphRAG's 72–83% comprehensiveness win rates over vector RAG demonstrate that hierarchical graph summarization genuinely solves the global sensemaking problem that flat retrieval cannot address. For the 10K–100K node graphs typical in enterprise deployments, community detection adds seconds of compute to pipelines dominated by hours of LLM inference, making the optimization frontier clear: invest in incremental summary regeneration, not faster graph partitioning.
